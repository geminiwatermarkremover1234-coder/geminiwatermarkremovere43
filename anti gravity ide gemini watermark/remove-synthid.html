What Is SynthID and How Does It Mark Your Images?

SynthID is an invisible watermarking system built by Google DeepMind. It marks AI-generated images, video, audio, and text so automated systems can identify their origin. Unlike a visible logo or stamp, SynthID operates beneath the surface — embedded directly into the frequency-domain patterns and pixel values of the image itself.

Because the SynthID watermark lives inside the pixel data, you can't get rid of it by cropping, resizing, compressing, or converting formats. Standard image editors don't even know it's there. The image looks perfectly normal to you, but a SynthID detector or checker can still pick up the hidden signal and flag the image as AI-generated.

How SynthID Watermarking Actually Works

Google DeepMind designed SynthID to be resilient. When an AI model like Gemini generates an image, SynthID embeds a unique digital signature into the image's frequency layers. Think of it like a fingerprint woven into the texture of every pixel — invisible to you, but readable by detection tools.

To actually disrupt a SynthID watermark, you need to work at the same level where it was placed — the frequency domain. That's what our SynthID watermark remover does. We apply tiny, structured perturbations across the image's pixel values. These changes are far too small to see (around 0.0005 to 0.003 of the pixel value range), but they're enough to scramble the watermark pattern that detection tools rely on.

Can You Tell If Your Image Has a SynthID Watermark?

Short answer: not by looking at it. SynthID watermarks are invisible. You won't spot them by zooming in, adjusting brightness, or running the image through a photo editor. The watermark doesn't change colors, add noise, or leave any visible trace.

If you generated the image using Google Gemini or another Google AI model, it's safe to assume SynthID is present. Google applies it automatically during the generation process. Our SynthID remover runs the cleanup locally either way, so you're covered whether the watermark is there or not — no separate detection step needed.
